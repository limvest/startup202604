# SOLARMAN-CN (solarman.cn) — The Domestic Installer CRM Hidden Inside a Monitoring Platform

**Entrant Type:** Independent (domestic operating branch of IGEN Tech / SOLARMAN global)
**Primary Segment:** Installer SaaS / CRM / Marketplace (domestic China)
**Founded:** Domestic China operations co-incident with IGEN Tech incorporation (2013); solarman.cn current form developed through 2016–2021 distributed boom
**Status:** Active

## Confidence Grade: ★3/★5

This profile analyzes the domestic Chinese solarman.cn platform as analytically distinct from the global SOLARMAN brand (covered separately in solarman-igen-tech.md). The distinction matters because the domestic platform's installer marketplace and CRM functions are not prominently featured in IGEN Tech's international investor communications, which emphasize monitoring scale and OEM partnerships. Data for this profile comes from direct website observation, platform documentation, and OEM white-label program materials — all company-originated. No independent user metrics, revenue figures, or market share data are available for the domestic-specific operation. Confidence is therefore constrained to structural and functional observations.

## Founding Narrative

SOLARMAN's parent company IGEN Tech (无锡英臻科技股份有限公司) was incorporated in 2013 in Wuxi, Jiangsu, but the domestic Chinese installer market was not the company's primary strategic focus in its first phase. The early SOLARMAN proposition was an OEM-agnostic monitoring backend for inverter manufacturers — a B2B hardware-and-cloud sale to inverter OEMs who would then distribute the monitoring solution to installers. This positioning made the international market as natural an expansion target as the domestic one: wherever Growatt, Goodwe, or Chint sold inverters, SOLARMAN loggers and the monitoring app could follow.

The domestic China platform, solarman.cn, evolved under a different dynamic than the international solarmanpv.com. China's 10,000+ small EPC and installation firms — the distributed-PV installer long tail — faced a business management problem that pure monitoring did not solve. These firms needed tools to acquire residential customers, generate solar installation proposals, manage project timelines, track receivables from grid interconnection subsidies, and manage after-sales O&M obligations. This is a CRM and business workflow problem, not a panel-monitoring problem. SOLARMAN's domestic platform gradually absorbed these functions — proposal generation, customer management, installer-to-manufacturer channel sales of hardware — creating a product that international investor communications would describe as "monitoring" but domestic installers would recognize as their primary business management tool (★3 confidence — functional observation from platform documentation, no internal product roadmap available).

The policy environment that made the domestic CRM evolution necessary was 531新政 (May 2018), which eliminated most new distributed solar subsidies overnight and forced the installer population to consolidate dramatically. The installers who survived the 531 shakeout were those who could operate efficiently — converting leads faster, managing O&M workflows with fewer people, tracking subsidy receivables precisely. A monitoring platform that added CRM and workflow functions became meaningfully more valuable post-531 than pre-531, because the surviving installer needed software productivity gains to compensate for compressed project margins (★3 confidence — structural inference, no pre/post-531 platform data available).

## Business Model Evolution

The domestic solarman.cn platform operates across three distinct revenue layers that are bundled into the installer relationship. The first is hardware: data loggers, WiFi sticks, and cellular gateways sold through the installer channel or directly to installers. Hardware margin is low and commoditizing, but the sale creates an installed base of connected devices that generate monitoring data — the raw material for everything that follows. The second layer is software subscriptions: SOLARMAN Business (the professional installer portal) provides multi-site monitoring dashboards, alarm routing, work-order ticketing, and O&M scheduling. This is the classic SaaS layer on top of hardware.

The third and analytically most interesting layer is the installer marketplace function. solarman.cn operates as a channel through which component manufacturers — module brands, inverter brands, balance-of-system suppliers — reach installers. This functions as an in-platform B2B procurement discovery tool: an installer who uses SOLARMAN for monitoring can also use the platform to source components, compare supplier offers, and contact manufacturers. The platform earns referral fees or channel sales margin on these introductions. This is structurally analogous to what Zheguyun built with its installer workflow platform, but implemented within a monitoring-native product rather than a workflow-native one.

The white-label OEM program adds a fourth revenue stream: IGEN Tech provides the monitoring and management backend to 20+ country partners who resell or rebrand the platform domestically. From the domestic solarman.cn perspective, this white-label capability means the software developed for Chinese domestic installers (work order management, proposal tools, CRM) can be commercially repackaged for international markets at near-zero marginal software cost. The domestic platform thus functions as the R&D base for features that eventually appear in international white-label deployments — a product development subsidy where Chinese installer usage patterns fund international feature development (★3 confidence — OEM white-label program from official documentation, domestic-to-international development sequence is analytical inference).

## Hypothesis and Counter-Hypothesis

**Primary Hypothesis:** solarman.cn's domestic China platform is more accurately characterized as an installer CRM with embedded monitoring than as a monitoring platform with CRM features — and this positioning distinction explains why it competes directly with Zheguyun (workflow-first) rather than with iSolarCloud (monitoring-first) in the small-installer segment.

**Evidence For:**
1. The platform's Chinese-domestic-specific features — phone-number registration (not email), domestic subsidy tracking, installer proposal generation, component sourcing — are business workflow tools that monitoring-focused competitors do not provide at equivalent depth (★3 — feature observation from platform documentation).
2. The target of "China's 10,000+ small EPC firms with digitalization tools" is explicitly an installer-business-management pitch, not a monitoring pitch — SOLARMAN's own domestic market communications emphasize business efficiency for installers over panel performance data (★3 — marketing material observation).
3. The marketplace function (component sourcing through the installer portal) creates a revenue stream only coherent if installers use SOLARMAN as their primary business platform, not merely as a monitoring backend they check occasionally (★3 — structural inference from product design).

**Counter-Hypothesis:** The installer CRM framing overstates solarman.cn's domestic distinctiveness — the CRM and marketplace features are ancillary to the monitoring core, adoption of these features by domestic installers is low, and the platform's competitive position is fundamentally based on monitoring hardware distribution rather than SaaS workflow value.

**Evidence Against Counter:** The hardware-distribution-first counter-hypothesis cannot be fully refuted at this data grade because installer adoption rates for the CRM features are unquantifiable. However, three structural observations support the primary hypothesis over the counter: (1) SOLARMAN's OEM partner strategy already guarantees hardware distribution without requiring domestic installer CRM adoption — the CRM features represent incremental investment whose rationale only makes sense if installer stickiness through workflow is a deliberate strategic goal; (2) the explicit domestic-vs-international product bifurcation (solarman.cn vs. solarmanpv.com with different feature sets) indicates deliberate localization rather than feature bloat; (3) direct competition with Zheguyun in the domestic installer segment is documented in competitive analysis, and Zheguyun's model is explicitly workflow-first — a monitoring-first competitor would not be identified as Zheguyun's primary domestic rival.

**Verdict:** ★3/★5. The hypothesis is the most analytically interesting claim for this profile and is supported by structural inference, but requires installer-level usage data to confirm above ★3. The counter-hypothesis remains plausible given the hardware origin of the business.

## Korea Applicability

**Transferability: ★4/★5**

The domestic solarman.cn model — monitoring hardware as the customer-acquisition vector, installer CRM as the stickiness mechanism, and component marketplace as the monetization layer — is the most directly transferable business architecture among the four companies in this profile batch for a Korean market entrant.

Korea's residential and small-commercial installer base shares the core characteristics that made the solarman.cn model necessary in China: installers are small (typically 1–5 person operations), manage multiple unrelated customer relationships simultaneously, have poor receivables visibility on REC payments, and currently rely on inverter-OEM apps for monitoring with no integrated workflow tools. The hardware-first acquisition path is particularly applicable: selling a monitoring data logger to an installer at near-cost as a CRM adoption vehicle is a proven customer acquisition model that does not require the installer to change their procurement behavior. Once monitoring hardware is installed and the app is in daily use, expanding to proposal tools, customer management, and component procurement features follows the same product-led growth logic that solarman.cn has demonstrated domestically.

The critical advantage of the solarman.cn model for Korea over a pure-SaaS approach (comparable to Zheguyun) is that the hardware creates a switching cost the moment it is installed: replacing the monitoring logger requires physical site access, inverter reconfiguration, and customer communication. This is a structurally more durable form of installer lock-in than SaaS subscription alone. For a Korean market entrant that cannot achieve the brand recognition of KEPCO or established inverter manufacturers quickly, the hardware-anchored CRM model offers a path to durable installer relationships that pure-software models do not.

The transferability rating is ★4 rather than ★5 because the marketplace layer — connecting installers to component manufacturers through the platform — depends on sufficient installer scale to make the marketplace valuable to suppliers. Korea's installer base may be too small for marketplace economics to work at launch, requiring the entrant to establish monitoring and CRM penetration before activating marketplace revenue.

## Unresolved Questions

- What percentage of solarman.cn registered installer accounts actively use CRM and proposal generation features versus using the platform solely as a monitoring dashboard? This is the critical question for evaluating whether the CRM hypothesis is confirmed or the counter-hypothesis is correct.
- How does SOLARMAN-CN's domestic installer marketplace compete with established Chinese B2B procurement channels (1688.com, WeChat-based supplier groups) for component sourcing? If installers continue to source components outside the platform, the marketplace layer is not generating meaningful revenue or lock-in.
- Is there a meaningful revenue or user-scale difference between the domestic solarman.cn platform and the global solarmanpv.com — specifically, does the domestic platform generate proportionally more revenue than its international counterpart given China's size and SOLARMAN's monitoring hardware installed base there?
